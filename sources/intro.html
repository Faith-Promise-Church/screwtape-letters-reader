The Screwtape Letters is a short novel by C. S. Lewis, first published in 1942. Lewis was an Oxford scholar and one of the most widely read Christian writers of the twentieth century, and this is one of his most beloved and most unusual books.

The entire book is written as a series of letters from a senior demon named Screwtape to his young nephew Wormwood. Wormwood has been given his first assignment, which is to secure the soul of an ordinary man living in World War 2 era England, around the time of the German air attacks. Through these letters, Screwtape offers advice on how to tempt, distract, and slowly corrupt his human target.

What makes the book so clever is its upside-down point of view. Everything is told from the perspective of Hell. So when Screwtape speaks of “the Enemy,” he means God. When he is angry about failure, he means a human turning away from sin and toward goodness. Lewis asks us to read the whole thing backward, and in doing so he holds up a mirror to our own habits, excuses, and small daily compromises.

The result is funny, sharp, and especially, convicting. You will laugh at Screwtape’s schemes, and then realize he is describing temptations you recognize in yourself. That is the genius of the work. As you listen, remember that you are hearing the voice of a tempter. Read against the grain, and let Screwtape teach you, by his own twisted logic, what a good life actually looks like.
